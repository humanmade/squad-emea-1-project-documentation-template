# Estimation

We use a system of points to estimate the effort required to complete stories. This helps the team plan, prioritise, and understand how much work we can sustainably take on over time.

## Why estimate?

Estimates give product management a tool for prioritisation. A high-effort item might be pushed in favour of several smaller, higher-value ones.

Estimation also shows whether you've understood the work. If you're not confident estimating a story, say so. It often means the outcome isn't clear enough yet, and that's worth surfacing before work begins.

Estimates are **not**:

- A precise time commitment. Things sometimes take longer than expected, and that's OK
- A measure of individual output. Different engineers approach the same task differently, and that doesn't change how it should be pointed

Over time, the team builds up a picture of its **velocity** — how many points we typically complete in a sprint. We use that to reflect on our own performance and plan sustainably.

While estimates aren't a measure of time, they do set a rough expectation of effort. If the reality feels way off from the estimate, it's worth discussing.

## How we estimate

Story points are an **abstract unit** that captures both effort and complexity. A small, well-understood task scores low. A task that involves significant unknowns, coordination, or technical complexity scores higher, even if the raw work time might seem similar.

We use the modified Fibonacci scale: **1, 3, 5, 8, 13**.

These numbers are intentionally non-linear. The gaps reflect the increasing uncertainty of larger, more complex work. Don't try to map them precisely to days as that defeats the purpose. Over time, as a team you'll develop a shared intuition for what each value feels like relative to the work you do together.

When an item scores an 8 or 13, look for ways to break it down into smaller, better-understood pieces.

A good way to avoid anchoring on time is to estimate relatively. Take a story the team knows well as a reference point and ask whether this new piece of work is bigger, smaller, or about the same.

We estimate as a group, not individually. Group estimation surfaces different perspectives, challenges assumptions, and deepens everyone's understanding of the work. We use planning poker in Jira to facilitate this.

## When to estimate

Ideally, estimation happens during refinement sessions with the whole team present. Having product management (and where relevant, design or QA) available to answer questions makes for better estimates. If that's not possible, avoid estimating alone. At minimum, do it with at least one other person.

## Sales and pre-project estimation

Estimating before a project starts is a different challenge. There are no tickets, no detailed acceptance criteria, and often significant unknowns. Accept that imprecision upfront rather than trying to engineer false precision out of it.

At this stage, work at the level of epics and build out a rough roadmap across sprints. Use T-shirt sizing (S, M, L, XL) rather than story points to reflect the higher level of uncertainty. Size epics relative to each other and think about what could realistically fit in a sprint.

This kind of estimate is always a conversation. We're often working to fixed budgets, so the goal is to work closely with the sales team to understand what's feasible for the price. An estimate at this stage is an input to that discussion, not a final answer.
